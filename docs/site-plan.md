## The main idea

Your slideshow is wonderful, but a slideshow only goes one way — front to back. A teacher looking for, say, a hands-on activity about play for second graders has to scroll through everything to find it. So I rebuilt the same material as a **searchable library**: every slide becomes its own entry that can be searched and filtered.

A teacher can now:

- **Search** for a word, a person, or an idea ("Octavia Butler," "brave space," "banned books").
- **Filter** by topic (the same sections you already have), by type (activity, thinking routine, quote, reading, and so on), and by grade level (early childhood through higher ed).
- **Open any entry** to read it in full, see who it quotes, and follow its links.

## Your slides are all still there — with the links working

This was the part I most wanted to get right. Your slides are full of links — videos, articles, websites, the real resources. I made sure **none of those got lost**. There's an "All Slides" page with every slide, and when you open one, all the links on it are clickable, right where they appear. (Earlier you spotted that a couple of video links — Vandana Shiva's 7-minute video, the Mari Copeny video — weren't clickable. That's fixed; every link in the whole deck is now live.)

I also kept the look and feel of your slides — the bold red, black, and turquoise, the hand-lettered title, your ARTWAYS / PLAYWAYS logo. The website wears your style, not a generic one.

## The best part: you keep working in Google Slides

You're clearly great with Google Slides, so I set this up so **you never have to touch the website itself**. You keep editing your slideshow exactly as you do now. When you want the website updated, you (or I) export the slideshow and run one quick step, and the website refreshes — new slides, new links, and all. Google Slides stays your workspace; the website just mirrors it.

## A couple of honest notes

- **It's a draft.** Some wording, groupings, and tags are my best guess from reading your slides — you'll know where I got something slightly wrong.
- **Images and permissions.** Many images in the slideshow come from other people (photos of activists, news pictures, other artists' work). That's completely normal for a teaching slideshow shared in class — but now that this is a public website anyone can find, some of those images may need permission, a credit, or a swap to one that's free to use. We don't have to solve this today, but it's worth a plan before we share the link widely. I can make you a list of which images are involved.

1. **Who is this for?** Your teacher-candidates and students, fellow educators everywhere, or the general public? That shapes the tone and what we put front and center.
2. **Does the look feel like *you*?** Too bold, just right, or should some parts be calmer?

## Ideas I think could make it even better

- **A few interactive pieces.** Your "Spectrum of Action" practically begs to be a slider a teacher can move themselves along, and your Culturally Responsive Curriculum Scorecard could become a fill-in tool that adds up a score. These would turn passive slides into things a teacher *does*.
- **A "Voices" page.** You cite so many powerful people (bell hooks, Joanna Macy, Favianna Rodriguez, Mari Copeny…). A browsable wall of them, each linking to every resource that mentions them, could be a beautiful entry point.
- **Readability and access.** A pass to make sure text is easy to read for everyone (good color contrast, descriptions on images for screen readers) — important for an equity-focused site.
- **A short welcome/orientation** for first-time visitors, so a teacher who lands on it knows where to start.

No rush on any of this — mostly I'd love your reaction to the overall direction, and your answers to the questions above so I can tune it.
